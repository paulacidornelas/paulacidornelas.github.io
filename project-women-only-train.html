What's at Stake: 'Women-Only' Train Passenger Cars in Brazil

During my exchange term at UC Berkeley, I had the opportunity to reflect more deeply on equity in transportation. One issue that stood out to me was the proposal of "women-only" train passenger cars in Brazil — a measure that, while seemingly simple, reveals the complex tensions between safety, equity, and public policy.

The Environmental Issue: Transportation Equity

Equity has significant importance in transportation. As Litman (2002) observes, "Equity refers to the fairness with which impacts (benefits and costs) are distributed." Transport projects may appear to benefit everyone equally, but men's and women's experiences with transport systems differ greatly, especially when it comes to safety and personal security.

The Scale of the Problem

According to a 2015 survey conducted in São Paulo by Datafolha, 35% of women said they had been sexually harassed while using public transportation.

One proposed solution, implemented in several countries including Brazil, is the controversial "women-only" passenger car. However, even among women, opinions on its effectiveness are divided.

Stakeholders and Special Interest Groups

In Brazil, several politicians have supported the "women-only" passenger car, partly because it's a short-term measure that brings political visibility and doesn't require significant investment. On the other hand, most transit operators are against the idea. Brazil's subway systems are already overcrowded, especially during peak hours, and setting aside a dedicated car for women could worsen this. Additionally, enforcing the rule would require more station staff and operational costs.

Beyond politicians and transport companies, it's important to consider what women themselves think. Interestingly, some women's groups affiliated with left-wing parties support the measure, while several autonomous feminist groups strongly oppose it.

Groups in Favor (e.g., Juntas, Movimento Mulheres em Luta)

  • Efficient and fast solution
  • Reduces harassment and sexual aggression
  • Increases safety and comfort for women

Groups Against (e.g., Marcha Mundial das Mulheres, Tática Feminista)

  • Increases gender segregation
  • Shifts blame to women in non-segregated cars
  • Doesn't address root causes of male violence

The "Face-Off" and Common Ground

As the comparison above shows, these groups diverge in their goals: improving immediate safety vs. addressing structural violence. On the one hand, creating a separate space for women doesn't solve the problem of sexual harassment, which is rooted in broader cultural norms.

Investing in education and respect is essential to prevent not only violence in public transportation but in all spheres.

Silva (2017)

On the other hand, cultural change takes time, and some action is necessary in the short term to reduce harm (Couto et al., 2019).

Despite the disagreements, both sides recognize the urgency of the problem and want to improve conditions for women in public transit. A possible point of common ground could be a medium-term strategy that avoids isolating women while actively addressing aggressor behavior.

Potential Solution: The Panic Button App

One promising example is a "panic button" system implemented in the city of Fortaleza. Through a mobile app, victims can press a virtual button labeled "Report Sexual Harassment," enter the vehicle number and time, and the transit company will send footage from 30 minutes before and after the incident to the police. The video can then support a formal complaint.

For those who support "women-only" cars: The panic button is an alternative that increases safety without separating passengers. It also extends protection to stations and other parts of the journey.

For those who oppose segregation: The panic button offers a non-isolating strategy that focuses on accountability rather than avoidance. Still, it's not a perfect fix — women must remain alert, and systemic issues like overcrowding and lack of enforcement remain.

Final Thoughts

This project was a chance to reflect on how complex transportation equity can be, especially when it comes to gender. Solutions that look simple often carry deeper social consequences. While "women-only" cars may provide a sense of safety for some, they risk reinforcing the idea that the burden is on women to avoid violence, rather than on society to prevent it.

Key Takeaway

Addressing this problem requires both short-term harm reduction and long-term cultural transformation. Strategies like the panic button may not be perfect, but they point toward a more inclusive, systemic approach to public safety — one that protects without isolating.
